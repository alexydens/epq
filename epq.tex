  \section{Introduction}
These days, trust and security over the internet is becoming more and more
essential. From communicating with banks, to managing sensitive information in a
business, to communicating privately over a service such as WhatsApp, two things
are always necessary: being able to verfiy you are who you say you are, and
being able to send a message that cannot be intercepted and understood in
transit. To accomplish this goal, digital cryptography has had to make several
crucial advances over the last 70 years, but it has been far from smooth
sailing. With so much valuable information at stake, and the risks of giving
people absolute secrecy, research into digital cryptography has marked by
frequently changing attitudes, standards and restrictions. The effect of these
external factors on this field of study, and the implications some of the
advances in it have yielded will be the focus of this document.

  \section{Initial State}
When talking about the advancements in digital cryptography, it is useful to
talk about the state of cryptography before any major advancements in digital
cryptography, and why crytography is necessary when communicating over networks.
  \subsection{Cryptography}
By the time the first computers were being built, cryptography had not advanced
much since the second world war. The most secure method of encryption was to
create a suitably convoluted cipher machine, which had a good balance between
ease of use, number of possible keys, and ease of key distribution.

When computers in the modern sense (programmable, Turing-complete machines)
first became available, they provided more or less equal benefits to
cryptographer and cryptanalyst. The cryptographer could now afford to make far
more convoluted algorithms to encrypt their messages, but the cryptanalyst could
just as easily work through far more combinations quickly to test possible
outcomes. The main advance came with ciphers containing large keys, such as the
vernam cipher, where, as long as the key is equivalent in length to the original
message, every byte of the message can be bitwise-XORed with the corresponding
byte in the key, creating a theoretically perfect cipher. The obvious
disadvantage to this system is that the key is now just as difficult to securely
transmit as the message itself.
  \subsection{Networks}
The issues with computer networks are quite simple, especially for the internet
in its current form. In a computer network, packets are routed through a large
number of individual devices and cables, which are all potential points of
attack for a person who wishes to intercept a communication. This was less of an
issue initially (in the 1940s and 50s), where computer networks tended to only
connect devices within a specific organisation (although wire tapping was still
possible), but became an unavoidable issue in from 1969 onwards, at which point
the ARPANet, and later the internet, connected many different sites and
organisations, and eventually households and mobile devices. As such,
intercepting communications has been a reasonably trivial goal for the majority
of the time computers have been able to communicate with each other.

This created two problems that could be solved through cryptography: trust and
security. Firstly, there was the issue that if any person could intercept a
communication, and anybody could send a communication, how can you be verify the
origin of the messages you recieve? Equally, how can you verify that the
messages you recieve haven't been tampered with on their way to you? Secondly,
if anyone can intercept your communications, there was no way to send a message
in secret without being sure someone other than the intended recipient had read
it.
