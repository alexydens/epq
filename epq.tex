  \section{Introduction}
These days, trust and security over the internet is becoming more and more
essential. From communicating with banks, to managing sensitive information in a
business, to communicating privately over a service such as WhatsApp, two things
are always necessary: being able to verfiy you are who you say you are, and
being able to send a message that cannot be intercepted and understood in
transit. To accomplish this goal, digital cryptography has had to make several
crucial advances over the last 70 years, but it has been far from smooth
sailing. With so much valuable information at stake, and the risks of giving
people absolute secrecy, research into digital cryptography has marked by
frequently changing attitudes, standards and restrictions. The effect of these
external factors on this field of study, and the implications some of the
advances in it have yielded will be the focus of this document.

  \section{Symmetric Encryption}
When computers in their current form first became commercially available,
cryptography had not advanced much since the second world war. The most common
type of cipher in use would involve a cipher machine, which would work in the
following way. Firstly, a key would have to be agreed upon that was sufficiently
long such that it couldn't be brute forced and sufficiently random such that it
couldn't be guessed. Next, the sender would set up the cipher machine, which
would consist of a convoluted circuit which changed as it encrypted the message,
with the agreed upon key. After that, the sender would type the message
character by character, into the machine, and each time a character was
inputted, the corresponding enciphered character would be outputted. In this
way, the message could be encrypted and sent to the reciever, who would repeat
the process in reverse, using the same key.

This process had two major issues. Firstly, to create a cipher machine that was
convienient to use and maintain, there was a limit on how convoluted you could
make the circuit, and hence a limit on how secure your machine could be.
Secondly, this method requires both sender and reciever to agree on a secret
key beforehand. If the sender and reciever can't communicate securely without
encryption, they won't be able to securely share their key, and hence can't use
encryption. The only way to ensure secrecy was to deliver keys in person, which
is clearly not ideal.

On computers, the process was more or less the same. The sender and reciever
agree on a key, the sender uses the key and an algorithm to encode the message,
and the reciever uses the same key and the algorithm in reverse to decode the
message. However, the previous limit on the complexity of a machine was more or
less removed, as implementing a complex algorithm on a computer requires
negligible increases in physical space compared to a simple one, and doesn't
require any extra moving parts that need to be maintained.

As such, initial breakthroughs in the field of digital computing came in the
form of better ``machines", most notably the Vernam cipher. To apply the Vernam
cipher, where a bitwise-XOR is applied to the key and the message to get the
ciphertext, and then the key and the ciphertext to get the plain text. As long
as the key is truly random and equal in size to the cipher text, this offers
perfect secrecy, and so the problem with this cipher rests only in the
requirement for an agreed upon key, made worse by the fact that the length of
the key must be at least the length of the message.
